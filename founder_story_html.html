Why I Built RapidPM

The £50m Rescue That Made Me Realize Something Had to Change

I was brought in to rescue a failing global ERP project. Within two weeks, I escalated it to RED status.

The problem wasn't the technology. It wasn't the team. It wasn't even the consultancy running it.

The problem was that nobody could find anything.

Project plan? PowerPoint slides. Requirements? Scattered across Excel files held by different people - none matching. Templates? Hundreds existed, but nobody used them. Everyone was making it up on the spot.

Result: A 4x estimation error (500 man-days estimated, 2,000 actually needed) and £5m overspent before I arrived.

This wasn't an isolated incident. Across 25+ years managing projects for global organizations, I saw the same pattern everywhere:

Three Case Studies From My Career

Case Study 1: The £50m ERP Rescue - Big 4 Consultancy, No Process

The Situation:
Global company. Oracle JDE implementation. 140 people across 9 countries. £50m+ budget. Run by a Big 4 consultancy. Brought in as rescue PM.

What I Found:

  • Project plan? PowerPoint slides with milestones. No stage gates.
  • Requirements? Four key people (Solution Architect, Programme Manager, 2 Project Managers) each had different Excel spreadsheets. None matched.
  • Templates? They claimed none existed. When challenged, dumped hundreds of random templates from past projects. Never used any of them. Created documents on the spot.

The 4x Estimation Error:
Consultancy estimated 500 man-days of development. After collating scattered requirements, actual: 2,000 man-days. Nobody had a single source of truth.

The Cost:

  • £750,000 underestimated on development alone
  • £5m (30%) project overspend before rescue
  • 140 people × 3 hours/week wasted = 10,920 hours over 6 months = £546,000 lost

The Fix:
Created unified product backlog, established stage gates, introduced standard templates people actually used. Successfully delivered for 3,000+ users. Saved £5m.

Case Study 2: Global D365 Rollout - Even the Best Companies Waste!

The Situation:
Most organized company I'd worked at. Solid PMO structure already existed. D365 implementation for multiple business units.

The Problems (Despite Good Structure):

  • Templates stored in multiple SharePoint sites, team sites, OneDrive folders
  • No version control - nobody knew which template was current
  • People asking "where's the latest template?" constantly
  • Hours per month wasted on template/version confusion, finding documents

The Cost:

  • Core team: ~15 people
  • 1 hour/week × 15 people = 15 hours per week wasted
  • At £62.50/hour = £937/week lost to document confusion alone
  • On ONE project. At the MOST organized company.

The Lesson:
If the best-run company with solid PMO still loses £1k per week on project documentation logistics, what's happening at average companies?

Case Study 3: Finance ERP - £7,500 Lost Creating One Document

The Situation:
UK company deploying finance ERP system. Project manager needed to create project charter.

The Problem:

  • No standard templates available
  • PM Googled templates online
  • Found multiple different versions, by multiple methodologies
  • Spent 3 weeks (120 hours) cobbling together their own versions and filling it in

The Cost:

  • 120 hours at £500/day = £7,500 wasted on just creating a subset of suitable templates that stakeholders could approve
  • Loss of credibility

The Pattern:
This happens on EVERY project. PMs waste days recreating templates that should be standardized, and should all work together as a coherent methodology so that there are no gaps or confusing overlap. Then wonder why projects fail.

Why Existing Tools Don't Solve This

I tried Monday.com. Asana. Jira. Notion. ClickUp.

They're all task management tools with document attachments bolted on.

They treat documents as afterthoughts.

But here's the reality: Projects succeed or fail based on the quality of your information.

You can have the best task list in the world, but if:

  • Your business case is weak → No budget approval
  • Your risk register is missing → Surprises kill you
  • Your technical specs are unclear → Development builds the wrong thing
  • Your project charter is incomplete → Stakeholders disengage
  • Your requirements are scattered → You get 4x estimation errors

The task management is the easy part. The documentation is what matters.
